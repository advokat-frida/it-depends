# Universal Core pairing review

Reviewed: 2026-07-23

## Result

**PASS: 144 of 144 Scenario/Missing Detail combinations read naturally without a compatibility filter.**

This is a narrative-coherence finding, not a legal conclusion and not a promise that every pair creates the same amount of drama. A valid pair may strengthen the proposal, weaken it, or leave a player’s call unchanged. The teaching requirement is narrower: the revealed fact must be understandable, decision-relevant enough to discuss, and incapable of inventing missing architecture.

## Why the first architecture was rejected

The former twelve dual-use cards produced 132 non-self ordered combinations. A fresh human audit found:

- 96 natural combinations;
- 13 strained combinations; and
- 23 combinations that required the room to invent a vendor, account, model, camera, region, workaround, or other absent component.

The old code-level domain flag therefore overstated compatibility. It proved only that every card claimed the same domain. It did not prove that the prose traveled. That architecture is superseded.

## Universal writing contract

Every Core Missing Detail may rely only on facts guaranteed by every Scenario:

- an organization is considering a proposal;
- data, an output, or both are involved;
- some version of the proposal was reviewed;
- the proposal has an intended reach, purpose, operating life, and owner;
- people can access underlying data;
- evidence supports the decision; and
- the proposal can be maintained, changed, or ended.

A Core detail fails if the room has to add “imagine there is also a vendor/account/model/camera/region…” before it makes sense. Failed details must be rewritten in neutral language or moved to a future tagged specialist pack.

## The twelve Missing Details

| Axis | Risk fact | Safeguard |
|---|---|---|
| Retention | **Twenty years later:** the organization keeps the data for 20 years after active use ends. | **The timer is real:** the approved limit is automatically enforced across controlled copies. |
| Access | **The shared key:** a shared group can view/export underlying data without individual logs. | **Names on the door:** access is individual and every view/export is logged. |
| Scope | **Default for everyone:** the review covered only a limited slice of the proposal’s actual reach. | **The fence holds:** technical limits hold the proposal inside the reviewed people, data, places, and duration. |
| Purpose | **The future-use drawer:** data and outputs flow into unrelated future work. | **Ask again:** another organizational use requires a fresh review and deliberate configuration change. |
| Ownership | **Nobody owns Tuesday:** ownership ends at launch; settings, access, correction, and deletion are orphaned. | **Someone owns Tuesday:** a named team owns and periodically tests those controls after launch. |
| Evidence | **Tested somewhere else:** approval evidence missed real data, operating conditions, or edge cases. | **The awkward cases made it in:** evidence was representative and remaining gaps were recorded. |

## Mechanical guardrails

The test suite separately:

- enumerates all 144 ordered cross-deck pairs;
- requires twelve unique Scenario IDs and art keys;
- requires twelve unique Missing Detail IDs and art keys;
- requires exactly six axes;
- requires one risk fact and one safeguard on every axis; and
- requires every Core detail to carry the `universal` scope marker.

Those checks protect deck structure. They do not replace the human audit above.

## Expansion rule

Universal Core remains small on purpose. A future law, incident-response, vendor, biometrics, AI, workplace, or research pack may use sharper details, but must declare compatibility tags and be dealt only with matching Scenarios. A future Pressure deck may add urgency, budget, executive, outage, or media constraints without pretending those are privacy facts.
